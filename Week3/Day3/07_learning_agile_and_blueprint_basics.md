## Part 1: Agile and Scrum – Quick Refresher

**Agile** is an approach to working in **small, iterative steps** with regular feedback.  
**Scrum** is one popular agile framework.

Some core ideas:
- Work in **short iterations** (sprints) with clear goals
- Keep a **prioritized backlog** of work items
- Hold regular **stand‑ups** and **review** meetings
- Get **feedback early and often** from stakeholders

In SAP projects this often means:
- Running **fit‑to‑standard** and design sessions in sprints
- Delivering **increments** of configuration and development
- Regularly reviewing with business owners

---

## Part 2: SAP Activate and Agile

SAP Activate is designed to be **compatible with agile ways of working**:

- Phases (Prepare, Explore, Realize, Deploy, Run) provide a **high‑level structure**
- Within phases (especially Explore and Realize) you can:
  - Use **sprints** to deliver pieces of configuration or design
  - Have regular **show‑and‑tell** sessions with business
  - Adjust the backlog based on feedback and findings

Examples:
- In **Explore**, you might run **sprints** of workshops:
  - Sprint 1: Order‑to‑cash processes
  - Sprint 2: Procure‑to‑pay
  - Sprint 3: Reporting and analytics
- In **Realize**, you might:
  - Configure and test a process in each sprint
  - Demo it to business users and refine if needed

## Part 3: What Is a Solution Blueprint?

A **solution blueprint** is a document (or set of documents) that describes the **future SAP solution** in enough detail so that it can be built and tested.

Typical contents:
- **Scope and assumptions**
- **Business process documentation** (to‑be process flows)
- **Organizational structures and master data**
- **Integration overview** (systems, interfaces, data flows)
- **Reporting and analytics needs**
- **High‑level security and roles**

In many projects, the blueprint:
- Is created or finalized during **Explore**
- Is used as a reference during **Realize**
- Is updated as changes are agreed

> Important: a blueprint should be **clear enough** to guide configuration and development,  
> but it does not need to describe every tiny detail that is better handled directly in configuration.

---

## Part 4: How Agile and Blueprints Work Together

Agile and blueprints are not opposites:

- The **blueprint** provides a **shared big picture** – what we are trying to build
- **Agile / Scrum** provides a **way of organizing work** and incorporating feedback

Possible approach:
- Use an initial blueprint draft to define **scope and direction**
- Use sprints to **refine** parts of the blueprint and then **configure and test**
- Keep the blueprint **alive** – update when important design decisions change

This connects back to:
- **Course 3** (discovery, workshops, customer engagement)
- **Course 4** (designing the solution and documenting it)
