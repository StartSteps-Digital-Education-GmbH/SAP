## Part 1: Why Communicating and Pitching Designs Matters

Design is not only about creating the **right solution** – it is also about **explaining** it so people can:
- Understand what is being proposed
- Challenge and improve it
- Approve funding and go‑live decisions

In real SAP projects you will often:
- Present the **solution concept** and roadmap
- Walk through key **process flows** and user journeys
- Discuss **risks, costs, and benefits**

Good communication:
- Builds **trust** between business and IT
- Reduces confusion and rework
- Helps align everyone on **scope and priorities**

---

## Part 2: Components of a Design Pitch

You can think of three main **components** when preparing a design pitch.

### 1. Presentation Materials

- Slides, diagrams, and demos that:
  - Show **end‑to‑end processes**
  - Highlight key **features and benefits**
  - Use clear **visuals** (no tiny text walls)
- Often include:
  - Process diagrams
  - System landscape diagrams
  - Screenshots or mockups (e.g. Fiori apps)

### 2. Stakeholder Engagement

- Know **who will be in the room**:
  - Executives and sponsors
  - Business process owners and key users
  - IT, security, and architecture
- Plan:
  - What each group cares about
  - What questions they are likely to ask
  - How you will **collect and respond to feedback**

### 3. Value Proposition

- Clear statement of **why this design is worth doing**:
  - Which business problems it solves
  - What improvements it brings (e.g. speed, quality, compliance, experience)
  - How it supports the **overall strategy**
- Often summarized in:
  - A short **value statement**
  - A few **supporting points** or examples

---

## Part 3: Elements of a Good Design Pitch

Typical **elements** in a design pitch include:

| Element | What it includes | Why it matters |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| **Executive summary** | 1–2 slides that explain the design and benefits at a high level | Busy stakeholders can quickly see what is being proposed |
| **Detailed design review** | Key processes, architecture, and scope; what is in / out | Ensures clarity and avoids hidden assumptions |
| **Q&A session** | Time for stakeholders to ask questions and challenge assumptions | Surfaces concerns early and builds trust |

Tips:
- Keep the **storyline simple**: problem → proposed solution → benefits → next steps
- Avoid jumping into technical detail **too early**
- Use **examples** and simple diagrams rather than long text

## Part 4: Link to Customer Engagement and Discovery

From **Course 3 – SAP Customer Engagement and Discovery** you have seen:
- How to run **discovery workshops**
- How to understand **customer needs**
- How to define **scope and value**

The design pitch is where you:
- Show how the design answers the **needs discovered** earlier
- Provide a **clear view** of what will be delivered
- Set expectations for the **implementation phases** (SAP Activate)

Think of it as a bridge between:
- **Discovery** (what the customer needs)
- **Implementation** (how you will deliver it)
