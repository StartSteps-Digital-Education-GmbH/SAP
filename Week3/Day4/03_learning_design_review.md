# Day 4: Design Review – Meetings, Documentation, and Approval
## How to Review and Approve an SAP Solution Design

In this learning file you will explore what a **design review** is, how it is run, and what outcomes it should produce in an SAP project.

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will:
- Explain the purpose of a **design review** in an SAP project
- Describe key components of a design review (meetings, documentation, feedback and recommendations)
- Identify typical **elements** of a design review (checklist, action items, approval process)
- Understand how design reviews reduce **project risk** and support **sign‑off**

## Part 1: What Is a Design Review?

A **design review** is a structured process where project stakeholders:
- Review the **proposed SAP solution design**
- Check that it meets **business and technical requirements**
- Identify **gaps, risks, and open questions**
- Decide whether the design is ready to move into **detailed build and test**

Design reviews happen at different levels:
- End‑to‑end solution design
- Specific process areas (e.g. order‑to‑cash, procure‑to‑pay)
- Technical topics (e.g. integration, security, reporting)

---

## Part 2: Components of a Design Review

Slides highlight three main **components**:

### 1. Review Meetings

- Structured sessions with:
  - Project team (functional, technical, project management)
  - Business stakeholders
  - IT/security and architecture
- Agenda usually includes:
  - Objectives of the review
  - Walk‑through of key design elements
  - Discussion of risks and open issues

### 2. Documentation

- Design documentation should be:
  - **Complete enough** to support decisions
  - Clear and **easy to navigate**
- Often includes:
  - Process diagrams and descriptions
  - System landscape and integration overview
  - Key configuration decisions
  - Non‑functional requirements (performance, security, availability)

### 3. Feedback and Recommendations

- Participants provide:
  - **Feedback** (what works, what is unclear)
  - **Recommendations** (changes or clarifications)
  - **Conditions for approval** (e.g. “approved if X is clarified”)

This feedback is captured and turned into **actions**.

---

## Part 3: Elements – Checklist, Action Items, Approval Process

Design reviews are more effective when they use clear **elements**:

| Element | What it includes | Why it matters |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| **Review checklist** | List of topics to cover (scope, processes, data, integrations, security, risks, testing impact, etc.) | Ensures nothing critical is missed |
| **Action items** | Specific tasks to address issues or questions, with owners and due dates | Turns feedback into concrete follow‑up work |
| **Approval process** | Clear decision (approved, approved with conditions, or not approved) and who signs off | Provides traceable sign‑off and aligns stakeholders |

Examples of checklist items:
- Are all **in‑scope processes** covered?
- Are **key integrations** identified and understood?
- Are **risks and assumptions** documented?
- Is there a **plan for testing** the design (later in the project)?

---

## Part 4: How Design Review Reduces Risk

A good design review helps:
- Catch misunderstandings **before** build and test
- Align business and IT on **scope and approach**
- Clarify **ownership** for decisions and follow‑ups

If you skip or rush design review, you risk:
- Rework and delays in Realize
- Missed requirements and unhappy users
- Surprises during testing or go‑live

As a Technology Consultant, you might:
- Help prepare **materials** for the review
- Walk through **technical aspects** (architecture, integration)
- Capture or follow up on **technical action items**
